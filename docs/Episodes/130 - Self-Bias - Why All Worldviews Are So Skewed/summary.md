## Overview

This episode explores self-bias—a concept Leo developed in his teenage years that fundamentally challenges how humans pursue truth and knowledge. Self-bias is the mind's natural tendency to favor its own intellectual positions while being blind to how its reasoning is circular and question-begging. Leo argues this is the core epistemological problem underlying all worldviews, ideologies, and belief systems, and that recognizing it is essential for genuine inquiry into reality.

## Key Insights

- **All worldviews are self-biased**: Every philosophy, religion, science, ideology, and belief system (including skepticism, pragmatism, and nihilism) is circular and defended through self-serving logic. Christians, scientists, atheists, and everyone else deny their own bias while claiming objectivity.

- **Self-bias is invisible to the biased**: The fundamental problem is that you cannot see your own self-bias from within it. Your mind will always convince you that you are objective and right, making self-bias virtually impossible to detect in yourself while easy to spot in others.

- **Survival creates bias**: Self-bias exists because admitting bias threatens your worldview and survival. There is no benefit to acknowledging that your core beliefs might be wrong—it creates doubt that erodes your entire foundation of meaning and security.

- **Evidence and logic are corrupted**: Because all evidence, proof, and reasoning are filtered through self-bias, they cannot be trusted as pathways to truth. Insane people, cult leaders, and those proven wrong by history all had "valid" logic and evidence supporting their positions.

- **This is the core epistemological problem**: The pursuit of truth requires impartiality and objectivity, but you cannot be impartial about something that defines your survival and identity. The judge cannot be unbiased when his survival depends on the verdict.

- **Consciousness inversely correlates with bias**: The less conscious a person is, the thicker their self-bias, the more extreme their defense mechanisms, and the more impossible it is to reach them through logic or evidence. High consciousness is required to see and transcend bias.

- **Historical examples reveal the pattern**: Dictators, colonizers, slave owners, nationalists, corporate lobbyists, and warring nations all genuinely believed they were right and justified. They weren't lying—they were blind to their own circular reasoning.

- **Pragmatism and anti-intellectualism are also self-biased**: The person who dismisses philosophy as "mental masturbation" and focuses only on survival and practical gains is equally self-biased; they've just chosen a different bias (toward practicality over theory).

- **Truth is radically threatening**: The actual truth with a capital T is so dangerous and threatening to all worldviews that people unconsciously construct elaborate defense mechanisms and rationalization schemes to protect against it.

- **This realization opens the door to awakening**: Once Leo saw this infinite black hole of groundlessness at the center of all philosophies, it opened his mind to the possibility of genuine inquiry beyond self-serving logic—ultimately leading him toward enlightenment and non-duality.

## Core Concepts

**Self-Bias**: The mind's natural tendency to favor its own intellectual positions, rationalize them through circular logic, and remain blind to this circularity. It's both a cognitive mechanism and a survival strategy rooted in ego.

**Question-Begging**: A logical fallacy where the conclusion is assumed in the premises. All worldviews are question-begging because they assume their foundational claims are true and then use those same assumptions to prove themselves.

**Epistemology**: The theory of human knowledge—how we know anything. Self-bias is the core problem of epistemology because it corrupts all our mechanisms for pursuing truth.

**Conflict of Interest / Recusal**: A legal principle recognizing that you cannot be objective when you have a "horse in the race." Leo argues this principle needs to extend beyond law to science, religion, business, and all domains of knowledge.

**The Self as Lens**: Your identity, survival needs, and conditioning act as a distorting lens through which you perceive reality. This lens is so pervasive you cannot step outside it from within it.

**Defense Mechanisms**: Psychological and intellectual strategies (rationalization, denial, dismissal, attack) used to protect a biased worldview from being questioned. The value of a defense mechanism depends on your denial that you're using it.

**Consciousness Correlation**: There is a direct relationship between low consciousness and thick self-bias. Higher consciousness allows you to see through bias; lower consciousness requires stronger defenses against questioning.

## Practical Takeaways

- **Audit your own circular reasoning**: Identify areas where you're assuming what you're trying to prove. Look for statements like "I know X is true because X works" or "I believe in Y because Y believers are right."

- **Notice when you deny bias**: Pay attention to moments when you insist you're objective or impartial. This denial is often a sign that you're deeply self-biased. Ask yourself what you have to lose by admitting bias in this area.

- **Seek out genuine steel-manning of opposing views**: Don't just read criticisms of positions you disagree with; find the strongest, most charitable articulation of views you oppose. See if you can understand why intelligent people hold them.

- **Question your survival-based beliefs**: Examine which of your core beliefs serve your survival, identity, or status. These are prime candidates for self-bias. Are you defending them because they're true or because you need them to be true?

- **Develop tolerance for groundlessness**: Practice sitting with the discomfort of not knowing. Notice how your mind frantically rushes to fill uncertainty with assumptions. Can you hold positions more lightly?

- **Recognize bias in yourself before others**: Make it a practice to catch your own bias-blindness before you critique others' biases. This is humbling but necessary for genuine inquiry.

- **Relinquish the need to be right**: Self-bias thrives on the need to defend being right. Practice being wrong, changing your mind, and updating your beliefs without making it a threat to your identity.
