## Core Concepts

**Classical Materialist Paradigm**: The default assumption that reality is objective, physical, made of atoms, operates mechanically like clockwork, and exists independently of observers. Shaped by Democritus, Aristotle, Descartes, Newton, and Laplace; it remains the unexamined foundation of most people's worldview despite being disproven by quantum mechanics.

**Wave Function Collapse**: The process by which an unobserved particle existing in infinite superposition (all possible states) precipitates into a single definite state through observation or measurement. Without collapse, nothing has definite form—all existence is potential.

**Quantum Superposition**: The principle that unobserved systems exist simultaneously in all possible states weighted by probability. This applies not just to electrons but to all phenomena including seemingly macroscopic objects.

**Non-Duality**: The understanding that reality is a singular, undivided consciousness in which all apparent separations (observer/observed, subject/object, self/other) are illusory constructs. Quantum mechanics mathematically describes what non-duality describes experientially.

**Observation/Measurement Problem**: The apparent paradox that quantum systems behave differently when measured versus unmeasured. The act of observation fundamentally affects and determines outcomes, proving measurement cannot be passive or objective.

**Strange Loop / Paradoxical Self-Reference**: A structure where inquiry circles back on itself—where the investigator (consciousness) discovers that consciousness itself is what it's investigating. Gödel's incompleteness theorem and recursive systems exemplify this principle.

**Visionary Science vs. Technical Science**: Visionary science integrates discovered phenomena with philosophy, metaphysics, and human consciousness to understand implications. Technical science executes experiments and builds technologies without addressing meaning, purpose, or philosophical ramifications.

**Quantum Entanglement**: When two particles become correlated such that measuring one instantaneously determines the state of the other, regardless of separation distance. Demonstrates that nature is fundamentally non-local and interconnected.
