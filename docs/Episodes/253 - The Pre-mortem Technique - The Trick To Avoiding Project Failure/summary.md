## Overview

The pre-mortem is a strategic planning technique that reverses the traditional post-mortem analysis by imagining project failure before it begins. Rather than analyzing what went wrong after a project ends, you visualize catastrophic failure at the outset, identify all possible causes (especially psychological ones), and develop solutions proactively. This method is applicable across all life domains—business, relationships, personal development, health, spirituality—and fundamentally shifts you into strategic thinking mode.

## Key Insights

- **Most failures are internal, not external.** People focus on external obstacles (economy, competition) but ignore their own psychological weaknesses, neuroses, and self-sabotage patterns—which are actually the primary causes of failure.

- **Psychological obstacles are subtle but critical.** These include paralysis by analysis, boredom, distraction, lack of discipline, inability to handle confrontation, perfectionism, and misaligned priorities—internal demons that derail projects far more often than external circumstances.

- **Strategic thinking requires both big-picture vision and meticulous detail-orientation.** You must operate simultaneously at the level of grand vision and specific technical execution; ignoring either level leads to failure.

- **Prioritization of failure causes matters.** Not all failure points are equally likely; rank them by probability and severity so you allocate resources and attention to the most dangerous threats first.

- **The pre-mortem creates emotional and psychological preparation.** By surfacing obstacles in advance, your mind can generate creative solutions and won't be caught off-guard; you enter a state of proactive rather than reactive planning.

- **This technique applies universally.** It works for starting businesses, careers, relationships, weight loss, travel plans, spiritual pursuits, education, life purpose—any significant project or life domain where failure is possible.

- **Most people never think this way.** The majority encounter problems only after they manifest; few engage in this level of forward-thinking strategic planning, which gives you a significant competitive advantage.

## Core Concepts

**Pre-mortem**: A strategic planning exercise where you imagine a project has failed catastrophically before it begins, then list all probable causes and develop solutions. Unlike a post-mortem (analysis after failure), it's preventative.

**Strategic Thinking**: The integration of holistic, big-picture vision with practical, concrete detail-work. It's about allocating resources wisely, thinking multiple steps ahead, and anticipating obstacles before they materialize. It's intuitive and logical simultaneously.

**Psychological Obstacles vs. External Obstacles**: The distinction between internal barriers (personality traits, neuroses, psychological patterns, discipline issues) and external barriers (market conditions, competition, economic factors). Internal obstacles are more common causes of failure but less frequently recognized.

**Cause Prioritization**: The practice of ranking failure causes by likelihood and severity, placing the most threatening obstacles at the top of your action list so you address what matters most.

## Practical Takeaways

1. **Select one significant project and conduct a 30-60 minute pre-mortem** using Leo's provided worksheet. Imagine the project failed spectacularly and list all possible causes—aim for 10-20 items, with heavy emphasis on psychological/internal causes.

2. **Identify your top 3 failure risks** by ranking causes by probability and severity. Be honest about your personal psychological patterns and weaknesses (e.g., tendency to get distracted, difficulty with delayed gratification, conflict avoidance).

3. **Develop concrete, realistic solutions for each major failure cause.** Don't just identify problems; brainstorm specific countermeasures and contingency plans for your highest-priority obstacles.

4. **Apply pre-mortems across all life domains:** your career trajectory (30-40 years), your entire life, your life purpose, relationships, financial goals, health goals, education, annual goals, hobbies, travel plans, and even spiritual pursuits.

5. **Cultivate the felt sense of strategic thinking.** Beyond the mechanical exercise, notice the psychological shift that occurs: you're being proactive, thinking ahead, allocating resources wisely, playing "10 moves ahead." This is the mindset to internalize.

6. **Be cautious of over-analysis.** Strategic thinking is not paralysis by excessive logic or overthinking every detail. Balance meticulous planning with intuition and action; don't let the pre-mortem become an excuse for inaction.

7. **Use your past failure experience.** If you've failed at weight loss, relationships, or projects before, leverage that experience to realistically predict psychological obstacles you'll face again and prepare solutions now.
