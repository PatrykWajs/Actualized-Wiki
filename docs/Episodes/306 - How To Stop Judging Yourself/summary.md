# How To Stop Judging Yourself

## Overview

This video explores the paradoxical mechanism by which judgments you make about others inevitably apply to yourself, creating unconscious constraints that sabotage your life. Leo demonstrates through multiple real-world examples how judgments become rigid rules your mind enforces consistently, and provides a powerful written exercise to uncover and address your hidden judgments.

## Key Insights

- **The Double-Edged Sword Principle**: Every judgment you make about another person automatically creates a rule that your mind applies to you, limiting your behavior and creating guilt/shame when you violate that rule.

- **Unconscious Operation**: Most people are completely unaware of how their past judgments are sabotaging their present circumstances—the connection between the judgment and its consequences is rarely obvious.

- **Rigidity and Inflexibility**: Judgments create arbitrary but rigid rules that prevent you from flowing naturally with life's circumstances, making you neurotic when you deviate from your own standards.

- **Judgment Extends to Positive Traits**: Even positive judgments (e.g., admiring someone's charisma) create problems by making you feel inadequate relative to an idealized standard you've established.

- **Systemic Life Sabotage**: The effects compound across all life domains—relationships, business, health, sexuality, finances—as you unconsciously avoid becoming the "judged" version of people.

- **Self-Image Integrity Drive**: Your mind compulsively maintains consistency with past judgments to preserve self-image integrity, making you sabotage success or happiness when it contradicts your judgment-based identity.

- **Childhood Origins**: Most significant judgments originate from parental conditioning and societal messaging internalized during formative years, then operate automatically for decades.

## Core Concepts

**Judgment as Law-Creation**: When you judge someone, you're not making a casual observation—you're creating a universal law that your mind treats as binding on yourself.

**The Consistency Principle**: Your mind prioritizes logical consistency with its established rules over your actual happiness or success, creating self-sabotage patterns.

**Disowning Reality**: Judgments function as a way of rejecting certain aspects of human experience as "not for me," which paradoxically draws you toward experiencing those very things.

**Neurotic Motivation**: When past judgments force you to avoid becoming the judged type, your actions become reactive avoidance rather than positive pursuit, creating unhealthy psychological patterns.

**Stream of Consciousness Integration**: Writing unfiltered lists of judgments allows unconscious patterns to surface where they can be examined and integrated.

## Practical Takeaways

1. **The Two-List Exercise**: Write an unfiltered stream-of-consciousness list of every judgment you've made about other people (100-300+ items), then create a separate list of every judgment you've made about yourself. Take 30+ minutes with no filtering.

2. **Identify High-Impact Judgments**: Star or check-mark the judgments from both lists that seem most likely to be creating neurotic patterns or self-sabotage in your life.

3. **Increase Judgment Awareness**: Practice mindfulness of how frequently you judge others—in conversations, jokes with friends, observations throughout your day. Notice subtle judgments, not just obvious ones.

4. **Trace the Double-Edge**: For each starred judgment, consciously identify how it's limiting you. (E.g., "I judge fat people → this creates insecurity about my own weight → this drives unhealthy gym motivation").

5. **Watch for Post-Exercise Insights**: After completing the exercise, maintain heightened awareness over the following week as automatic judgments become more visible to you.

6. **Work with One Judgment Deeply**: Once identified, examine one limiting judgment in detail—trace it to its origin, notice how it's constrained your choices, and consciously give yourself permission to violate that old rule.
