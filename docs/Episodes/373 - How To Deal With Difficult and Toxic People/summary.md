# How To Deal With Difficult & Toxic People

## Overview

This video addresses the critical issue of how to identify and handle toxic people in your life across all relationship categories. Leo argues that surrounding yourself with negative influences actively prevents self-actualization and personal fulfillment, and that the primary solution is to remove these people from your life entirely—though he also provides strategies for situations where complete removal isn't immediately possible.

## Key Insights

- **You are a sponge**: Your mind absorbs the negativity, beliefs, and behaviors of people around you, making negative relationships directly sabotage your potential regardless of your personal development level.

- **Jim Rohn's Averaging Principle**: You become the average of the five people you spend the most time with. Tracking actual hours spent with people in your life often reveals shocking patterns about who's influencing you most.

- **The Skewer Metaphor**: Just as in chess you can become trapped by strategic vulnerabilities, you become unable to cut toxic people from your life when you depend on them for financial security, companionship, employment, or emotional support.

- **Toxicity is a spectrum**: While it ranges from mild annoyance to abusive territory, even seemingly minor toxicities accumulate over time like cancer, metastasizing throughout your psychology, habits, and worldview.

- **Everyone can be cut**: Regardless of relationship closeness (boss, family, spouse, children), you must have firm boundaries. Those closest to you need *more* boundaries, not fewer, because they'll test limits.

- **Most people won't reform**: While some people may change after a serious boundary-setting conversation, most operate from such low consciousness that they're incorrigible and will repeatedly violate your values on autopilot.

- **Strategic independence is the solution**: Long-term freedom from toxic relationships requires building abundance in finances, relationships/sexuality, business, and friendships so you're never dependent on any single person.

- **Don't engage in the heat of the moment**: When someone becomes dramatic or angry, disengagement and walking away—through mindfulness and emotional non-reactivity—is far more effective than attempting to reason or defend yourself.

## Core Concepts

**Toxicity Categories**: Depression, negativity/limiting beliefs, dogmatism/closed-mindedness, anger-proneness/violence, drama, criminal activity, and addictions (hardcore or soft). The most dangerous contexts are boss/coworkers, intimate partners, family, and friends.

**The Sponge Effect**: Your subconscious mind automatically absorbs the emotional states, beliefs, and behavioral patterns of people you regularly interact with, whether you consciously intend to or not.

**Strategic Vulnerability**: Being unable to cut someone from your life isn't a moral failing—it indicates a strategic life position where you're dependent on that person for something critical (money, love, opportunity, emotional support).

**Consciousness Levels**: Toxic people operate from low consciousness states and run their lives "like animals"—on autopilot, seeking easy gratification, testing boundaries, and incapable of high-consciousness collaboration.

**The Chess Skewer**: A metaphor for being trapped: your king (core dependency) is in check, so you can't move your queen (the person you want to cut) without losing it. The solution is long-term repositioning.

**Boundary Enforcement**: Boundaries must have "teeth"—idle threats teach people your boundaries are empty. Consistency and willingness to enforce consequences are essential, especially with low-consciousness people.

## Practical Takeaways

- **Audit your top five associations**: Write down the five people you spend the most hours with per week. Honestly assess whether they're raising or lowering your standards and vision.

- **Identify your strategic vulnerabilities**: Recognize what keeps you dependent on toxic people (financial need, sexual/romantic need, business dependence, emotional support) and create a one-to-two-year plan to build independence in those areas.

- **Have a serious boundary-setting conversation**: If you haven't already, sit down with the toxic person and clearly communicate your boundaries, expectations, and values. Make clear that continued violations will result in separation. Observe if they take you seriously.

- **Reduce exposure if you can't eliminate it**: If you spend 20 hours weekly with someone toxic, reduce it to 10 or 5. Even partial reduction significantly decreases their negative influence based on the averaging principle.

- **Add positive counter-influences**: Since you may not immediately cut everyone out, deliberately add positive influences—mentors, self-help content, books, seminars, videos—to counterbalance negative in-person relationships.

- **Practice non-engagement in heated moments**: Use mindfulness and meditation to maintain emotional distance and equanimity when someone becomes dramatic or angry. Walk away, don't respond, don't feed the cycle.

- **Use difficult people as mirrors for self-improvement**: Rather than judging others, reframe toxic behavior as a reminder of your own potential blindspots and areas for growth. Focus on improving yourself rather than controlling them.
