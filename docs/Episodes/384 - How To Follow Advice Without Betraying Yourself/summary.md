# How To Follow Advice Without Betraying Yourself

## Overview

This video addresses the critical tension between receptivity to wisdom and personal autonomy when consuming self-development advice. Leo argues that while clear, forceful advice is valuable, followers must intelligently customize that guidance to align with their unique values, life circumstances, and circumstances rather than blindly adopting it wholesale or defensively rejecting it.

## Key Insights

- **Everyone has biases and values**: All advice—including Leo's—is filtered through the advisor's personal values, life experiences, and biases. No one can give you perfectly universal advice because everyone's circumstances differ.

- **Values determine life trajectory**: Your core values (roughly 10-15 fundamental principles) are what actually govern major life decisions and outcomes. Most people don't consciously know their own values, which creates problems when following others' advice that conflicts with their values.

- **Blind obedience betrays yourself**: Following advice wholesale without critical evaluation can lead you to adopt lifestyles (marriage, careers, etc.) that fundamentally misalign with who you are, resulting in dissatisfaction and unfulfillment.

- **Ego defenses masquerade as discernment**: People often use phrases like "this doesn't apply to me" or "you don't understand my situation" as unconscious ego defenses to avoid confronting uncomfortable truths, rather than as legitimate reasons to reject advice.

- **Walk the fine line between openness and autonomy**: You must simultaneously remain genuinely open-minded (willing to test advice), while maintaining yourself as the ultimate authority on what works for your life.

- **Judge by results, not by feeling**: The true test of whether advice works is measurable outcomes in your life (relationships, finances, health, growth), not whether the advice feels right or aligns with your current beliefs.

- **Fundamentals are universal, details are customizable**: Like a car, humans share basic foundational needs for success and happiness, but the specific expression of those fundamentals should be customized to your unique circumstances and values.

- **Consistent growth is the metric**: If you're truly following effective strategies, you should see noticeable expansion and improvement in your life within weeks and months, not years. Stagnation signals a problem with your strategy selection or implementation.

## Core Concepts

**Values**: Core principles and priorities that fundamentally shape life choices. These are often unconscious but universally present, and conflict between adopted advice and your actual values leads to unhappiness.

**Ego Defenses**: Psychological mechanisms that protect self-image by rejecting uncomfortable information or advice. These can masquerade as legitimate reasons to dismiss guidance, when they're actually avoidance mechanisms.

**Critical Thinking in Advice-Taking**: The balanced approach of remaining genuinely open to new ideas while maintaining discernment about what aligns with your life and values.

**Customization vs. Wholesale Adoption**: The principle that fundamental truths can be applied to everyone, but the specific implementation must be tailored to individual circumstances, values, and life context.

**Results-Based Evaluation**: Assessing whether advice is working based on measurable outcomes and tangible life improvements, not on theoretical agreement or emotional resonance with the ideas.

## Practical Takeaways

- **Identify your core values consciously**: Take time to articulate what you actually value in life (relationships, autonomy, creativity, security, growth, etc.) so you can evaluate advice against them rather than unconsciously adopting others' values.

- **Test advice experimentally**: Rather than immediately accepting or rejecting guidance, treat it as a testable hypothesis. Give new strategies genuine effort for several weeks or months and evaluate based on actual results.

- **Use the "one-year test"**: Assess whether you're genuinely growing and improving across life domains (career, relationships, health, finances, emotional state) year-over-year. If not, your strategy selection or values alignment is problematic.

- **Catch yourself using "uniqueness" as ego defense**: When you notice yourself saying "that won't work for me because I'm different," pause and honestly evaluate whether this is legitimate discernment or an ego defense protecting you from change.

- **Separate the fundamentals from the details**: Adopt the basic proven principles (sleep, exercise, responsibility, healthy relationships, financial literacy) while giving yourself freedom to customize how you implement them.

- **Maintain dual awareness**: Simultaneously hold the position of being genuinely open-minded to new information AND being the final authority on your life. These aren't contradictory; they're complementary.

- **Track measurable outcomes**: Don't rely on how advice "feels"—instead measure concrete improvements in your life across multiple domains (mood, success, relationships, health) to determine if your strategy is working.
