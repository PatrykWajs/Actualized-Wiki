## Overview

Leo introduces the personal mission statement as a critical tool for becoming principle-centered rather than externally-driven. A mission statement is a concise one to two-page document that consolidates your values, life purpose, strengths, zones of genius, and top goals. The core thesis is that reviewing this document for five minutes every morning fundamentally shifts you from being at the effect of the external world to being a creator who actively influences your reality.

## Key Insights

- **Principle-Centered Living:** Most people are centered in external things (work, relationships, pleasure) rather than internal principles, leaving them unstable and reactive to the world. A mission statement grounds you in your own values and purpose.

- **Inner World Influences Outer World:** Successful people shape their external reality through their internal clarity and values. Without a personal mission statement, the external world dictates your agenda through emails, crises, and distractions.

- **The Daily Practice is Non-Negotiable:** Reading your mission statement for five minutes every morning is not optional—it's essential for training your subconscious mind (the "elephant") to embody your principles throughout the day.

- **Boring Work, Massive Payoff:** Even though reviewing the same document daily becomes monotonous, the cumulative effect over months and years creates extraordinary discipline, integrity, and goal accomplishment.

- **It's a Living Document:** Your mission statement should be regularly edited and refined as you grow and gain clarity. Electronic format is preferable because it allows easy updates without the commitment of physical formats.

- **Specificity Matters:** General life purpose (abstract, unchanging) differs from specific life purpose (career/contribution-related). Having both provides grounding and direction simultaneously.

- **Mastery Over 10 Years:** Committing to mastering one skill or domain for a decade creates focus and prevents scattered effort. This is different from goals—it's a long-term commitment regardless of outcomes.

## Core Concepts

**Personal Mission Statement**
A concise written document (1-2 pages) listing your general purpose, specific life purpose, values, strengths, zones of genius, desired feelings, and top annual goals. Reviewed daily to keep you principle-centered and internally driven.

**Principle-Centered vs. External-Centered**
Stephen Covey's concept where you're either guided by your own internal principles or buffeted by external circumstances. A mission statement makes you principle-centered rather than reactive.

**Zone of Genius**
From Gay Hendricks' work, these are the activities where your unique talents, passions, and contributions align perfectly. Living from your zones of genius is higher-impact than operating from mere competence.

**Mastery**
A 10-year commitment to deeply learning and practicing a specific skill or domain, creating expertise and excellence that compounds over time.

**Values Clarification**
The process of identifying your top 10 values in order of importance. These are the principles that guide your decisions, relationships, and how you want to feel in life.

## Practical Takeaways

1. **Write Your Mission Statement** using the specific format provided: general purpose, specific life purpose, one-word mastery focus, top 10 values, top 5 desired feelings, top 5 strengths, zones of genius, and top 5-10 annual goals.

2. **Commit to Daily Reading** for five minutes every morning as part of your routine (after hygiene, before work). Treat it as non-negotiable, like brushing your teeth. Give yourself 30 days to build the habit.

3. **Use Electronic Format** for ease of editing and portability. Your mission statement is a living document that should be tweaked weekly or when major life insights emerge.

4. **Contemplate While Reading** rather than mechanically reviewing. Ask yourself: Is this really true? Is this the best wording? Can I refine this further?

5. **Take the Character Strengths Assessment** at authenticahappiness.com (Martin Seligman's positive psychology tool) to identify your top five strengths objectively before adding them to your statement.

6. **Identify Your Zones of Genius** by reading Gay Hendricks' *The Big Leap* or reflecting on activities where you lose track of time and create exceptional value with minimal effort.

7. **Use Your Mission Statement to Re-Center Throughout the Day** whenever external chaos (conflicts, crises, distractions) pulls you off-course. It's your anchor to your true priorities and purpose.
